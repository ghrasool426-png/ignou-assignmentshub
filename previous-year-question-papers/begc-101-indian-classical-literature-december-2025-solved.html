Q1 (iii) Bhavabhuti as a Dramatist

Bhavabhuti is one of the greatest Sanskrit dramatists, known for his intense emotional depth and philosophical seriousness. Unlike Kalidasa’s lyrical grace, Bhavabhuti focuses on human suffering, moral dilemmas and psychological realism. His major works include Malatimadhava, Mahaviracharita and Uttararamacharita. He excels in portraying Karuna Rasa and presents complex characters driven by inner conflict.

Q1 (vi) Sangam Literature

Sangam Literature refers to ancient Tamil literary works composed between 300 BCE and 300 CE. It is divided into Akam (love/inner life) and Puram (war/public life) poetry. These texts provide valuable insight into early Tamil society, ethics, politics and cultural life.

Q2 (i) Purpose of Dramatic Art according to Natyashastra

According to Bharatamuni’s Natyashastra, the primary purpose of dramatic art is to educate and entertain society. Drama serves as a mirror of life, presenting moral lessons through emotional engagement. The concept of Rasa lies at the core of Indian dramaturgy, allowing audiences to experience aesthetic pleasure while learning ethical values.

Q3 (i) Kalidasa’s Reworking of Shakuntala

Kalidasa’s Abhijnana Shakuntalam is a refined artistic transformation of the Shakuntala episode from the Mahabharata. While the epic presents the story in a direct narrative form, Kalidasa enhances it with emotional depth, romantic sensitivity and dramatic structure.

One major innovation is the introduction of Durvasa’s curse, which causes Dushyanta to forget Shakuntala. This change protects the king’s moral stature and shifts responsibility from deliberate neglect to fate. Kalidasa also softens Shakuntala’s character, portraying her as gentle, emotional and modest, thereby intensifying Shringara Rasa.

The ring motif symbolizes memory, destiny and recognition, adding dramatic suspense. Through separation and reunion, Kalidasa explores love, suffering and redemption, elevating the narrative into a timeless romantic masterpiece.

Q3 (ii) Draupadi’s Questions in the Kuru Court

During her humiliation in the Kuru court, Draupadi raises a profound moral question: whether Yudhishthira had the right to stake her after losing himself. This question exposes the ethical failure of the elders present in the court.

Draupadi’s argument challenges patriarchal authority and highlights the dignity and autonomy of women. Her courage, logical reasoning and moral clarity make her a powerful voice of justice. Even in extreme humiliation, she refuses to accept injustice, revealing her strength, intelligence and resilience.

Q3 (vi) Dharma as the Central Theme of the Mahabharata

Dharma is the central philosophical foundation of the Mahabharata. Rather than presenting a rigid moral code, the epic portrays dharma as complex, situational and deeply human. Characters constantly face moral dilemmas where every choice carries consequences.

Yudhishthira’s struggles, Krishna’s pragmatic guidance and Bhishma’s lifelong vow all demonstrate different interpretations of dharma. The epic suggests that righteousness is not absolute but must be understood through wisdom, compassion and responsibility.
